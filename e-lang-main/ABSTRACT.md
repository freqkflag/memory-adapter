# Abstract

**Background**: Emotional representation systems in research, therapy, and human-computer interaction typically rely on categorical emotion labels (e.g., "happy", "anxious") that assume neurotypical emotional processing and linguistic access. This creates barriers for neurodivergent individuals, particularly those with autism and ADHD, who may experience emotions as continuous, multidimensional, or pre-linguistic phenomena.

**Methods**: We developed E-Lang, a formal symbolic language for representing emotional experience prior to linguistic labeling. E-Lang encodes emotional states as vectors across eight continuous axes (Valence, Arousal, Agency, Safety, Certainty, Dominance, Attachment, Novelty) and provides operators for composition, context application, masking, and temporal transitions. The language was developed and validated through structured autoethnography by a neurodivergent researcher.

**Results**: E-Lang successfully captures emotional experience without requiring categorical labels, supports explicit modeling of masking behaviors, and maintains separation between representation and interpretation. The system demonstrates expressive adequacy for neurodivergent emotional reporting and reduces translation pressure between internal experience and external communication.

**Conclusions**: E-Lang provides a missing abstraction layer for emotional representation that is neurodiversity-affirming and resistant to coercive or surveillance-based use. It offers infrastructure for journaling, therapy-adjacent communication, research, and ethical human-AI interaction.

**Keywords**: emotional representation, neurodiversity, autism, ADHD, symbolic language, accessibility, human-computer interaction
